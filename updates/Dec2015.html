The 126th Editorial Board

Students gathered on the quiet green to show support with students at the University of Mizzou.

Now that production for the semester is over, I’m taking the time to write to you about some of what The Brown Daily Herald accomplished this semester and what the newly appointed 126th editorial board is hoping for in 2016.

In the past semester, we’ve uncovered a gender wage gap among Brown faculty. After the unfortunate suicide of a physics graduate student, The Herald probed the mental health resources available to students of all backgrounds on campus in a three-article series. And as race relations become more and more important on college campuses across the country, The Herald explored diversity in theatre at Brown.

The Herald was there when students stood in solidarity with students from the University of Missouri, when administrators responded to student outrage about the alleged assault of a visiting Dartmouth student by a DPS officer, when President Paxson released a draft of the University’s diversity and inclusion action plan, and when students occupied University Hall to draft their demands in response.

The Herald is always there to connect alumni with all the most important campus news.

Students worked in the Leung gallery in the campus center to draft a list of demands with regard to the University's diversity and inclusion action plan.

In the next year, The Herald will accomplish many things. The 126th editorial board will focus on digital initiatives by revamping the mobile version of our website, producing substantially more online-only content and re-structuring our social media and web teams.

The Herald also aims to become a more inclusive and culturally competent organization after the publication of two opinions columns that ignited criticism of The Herald on race and diversity. We aim to do so partially through our financial aid program. The Financial Aid Initiative ensures that every dedicated student can be involved, regardless of financial need. Through grants of up to $1,250 per semester to staff members with demonstrated financial need, the program allows students passionate about their work at The Herald to do so in place of a work-study job or other time-consuming, financially imposed burden.

In our 125th year, The Herald sets its sights on digitizing its entire archives. Making every paper published by The Herald available for free online is critical to our mission as a reflection of the student body and a historical document.
